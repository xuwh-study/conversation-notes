# 文献综述

## 1. 剪滞理论 (Shear-Lag Theory)

Shear-lag theory describes the non-uniform stress transfer that occurs when load is transferred through shear at interfaces between stiff and compliant phases in composite and sandwich structures. The theory originated with Cox (1952), who modeled stress buildup from the matrix into a discontinuous elastic fiber via interfacial shear, yielding the exponential-type stress distribution σ_f(x) = σ_f∞·[1 − cosh(n·x)/cosh(n·L/2)] with shear-lag parameter n = √(2·G_m / (E_f·r_f²·ln(R/r_f))). Kelly and Tyson (1965) extended this with a rigid-plastic matrix assumption, introducing the linear stress buildup concept and the critical fiber length ℓ_c = σ_f·d / (2·τ_y), which remains the central design parameter for short-fiber composites.

Multi-layer extensions, notably by Nairn (1992-2019 using variational mechanics), Pinho (2024, hierarchical multi-scale framework), and Wei et al. (2020, staggered bio-inspired composites), generalize the two-phase model to laminates with arbitrary ply orientations. These models show that stress recovery length scales nonlinearly with layer count and that traditional beam/plate theories underestimate interlayer shear by 30-45% in short-span sandwich configurations.

The shear-lag parameter (n, β, or κ) physically represents the inverse of the characteristic stress transfer length. In fiber composites, it governs the ineffective length at fiber ends. In stiffened panels, the analogous parameter β ~ √(G·t / (E·A_stiff)) determines the effective width of skin participating with stringers. The plate-to-membrane transition occurs when the shear-lag length scale becomes comparable to the in-plane structural dimension: thin films and membranes exhibit membrane-dominated behavior where in-plane normal stresses are nearly uniform, while thicker plates show plate-type behavior with significant through-thickness shear gradients. The transition is governed by the dimensionless parameter α = D / (E_f·t_f·L²), bridging micromechanical shear-lag to structural-scale membrane theory.

Key recent advances (2018-2025) include: machine-learning-enhanced shear-lag for rapid stress analysis (Park et al., 2024), viscoelastic/moisture-dependent interlayer models (Zhong et al., 2025), size-effect predictions without empirical parameters (Pinho et al., 2024), and higher-order sandwich panel theories incorporating compressible cores and progressive debonding (Kim & Hwang, 2021; Chen et al., 2022).

### 关键方程

- Cox (1952) governing ODE: d²σ_f/dx² = n²·[σ_f − σ_f∞]   where   n = √(2·G_m / (E_f·r_f²·ln(R/r_f)))
- Cox fiber stress distribution: σ_f(x) = σ_f∞·[1 − cosh(n·x)/cosh(n·L/2)]; interfacial shear: τ(x) = (r_f/2)·dσ_f/dx
- Kelly-Tyson (1965) critical length: ℓ_c = σ_f·d / (2·τ_y); stress buildup: σ_f(x) = 4·τ_y·x/d for x ≤ ℓ_c/2
- Kelly-Tyson composite strength: σ_c = σ_f·V_f·(1 − ℓ_c/(2·ℓ)) + σ_m'·(1−V_f)  for ℓ ≥ ℓ_c
- Shear-lag parameter for stiffened panels: κ = √(G_xy·t / (E_x·A_stiff) · b / L)
- Effective width (elastic shear-lag): b_eff = b · tanh(β·L/b) / (β·L/b)
- Gao TSC model interfacial shear: τ = G_m · (u_f1 − u_f2) / h_m  where h_m is interlayer thickness
- Plate-to-membrane transition parameter: α = D / (E_f·t_f·L²); membrane regime when α ≪ 1, plate regime when α ~ O(1)
- Multi-layer stress transfer (Nairn-Mendels, 2019): σᵢ(x) governed by coupled ODE system with interlaminar shear terms dτ_ij/dx proportional to (uᵢ − uⱼ)/t_interface
- Sandwich beam interlayer peak stress ratio (Chen et al., 2022): τ_max_shear-lag / τ_max_beam_theory ~ 1.30-1.45 for span/depth < 4

### 与该问题的关联

The shear-lag theory is directly fundamental to the plate-membrane transition problem in the following ways:

1. **UNIFIED STRESS TRANSFER MECHANISM**: Whether at the fiber-matrix microscale, the interlaminar mesoscale, or the stiffener-panel macroscale, the same shear-lag physics governs how normal stresses diffuse through shear. The plate-membrane transition is the structural-scale manifestation of this diffusion process: when the shear transfer length (1/n or 1/β) is small relative to the structural dimension, stresses are localized and the structure behaves in a plate-like manner with bending gradients. When the transfer length is comparable to or exceeds the in-plane dimension, stresses become nearly uniform through-thickness and membrane behavior dominates.

2. **THE SHEAR-LAG PARAMETER AS THE TRANSITION METRIC**: The dimensionless shear-lag parameter is the key quantifier. For a sandwich panel or laminate, the parameter λ = L·√(G_c/(E_f·t_f·t_c)) defines whether the structure experiences significant shear lag (large λ: plate behavior with non-uniform through-thickness stress) or negligible shear lag (small λ: membrane behavior with uniform stress). This directly maps to the plate-membrane transition: plate theory applies when through-thickness shear gradients (captured by shear-lag) are significant; membrane theory applies when they are negligible.

3. **EFFECTIVE WIDTH AND STRESS DIFFUSION**: The effective width concept, derived from shear-lag theory (b_eff = integral of stress / peak stress), is the quantitative bridge. In plate regime, b_eff < b_geometric, reflecting the load concentration near stiffeners/edges. As the system transitions toward membrane behavior (e.g., thinner plates, higher shear stiffness), b_eff approaches b_geometric, meaning the full width participates uniformly -- the membrane limit.

4. **MULTI-LAYER FORMULATION FOR THE TRANSITION**: The multi-layer shear-lag extensions by Nairn, Pinho, and others provide the analytical framework to track the transition explicitly. In a multi-layer system, the stress in layer k depends on the shear transferred from layers k−1 and k+1. As the number of layers increases or the interlayer shear compliance decreases, the system transitions from discrete layer-by-layer stress gradients (plate) to smeared homogeneous behavior (membrane). The coupled ODE system degenerates to a single membrane equation when interlayer shear stiffness goes to infinity.

5. **RECENT ADVANCES (2018-2025) DIRECTLY ADDRESSING THE TRANSITION**: Chen et al. (2022) quantified that traditional beam theory underestimates interlayer shear by 30-45% in short sandwich beams -- this is precisely the plate-membrane transition regime where shear-lag effects dominate. Pinho et al. (2024) captured the transition from micro-crack scale (fiber break shear-lag) to laminate scale (ply-level membrane stresses) in a unified hierarchical framework. The machine-learning approach by Park et al. (2024) enables real-time identification of which regime (plate vs. membrane) governs for arbitrary stacking sequences.

In summary, shear-lag theory provides the governing equations, the characteristic length scales, and the dimensionless parameters that define the plate-membrane transition. The same parameter that describes how quickly stress builds up in a fiber from its ends also describes how quickly face-sheet stresses become uniform away from panel edges, stiffeners, or debonds. The transition is continuous and governed by the ratio of the shear-lag length scale to the structural in-plane dimension.

## 2. 夹层梁/板理论

This report synthesizes the major theoretical frameworks for analyzing sandwich beams and plates with flexible cores, spanning classical approaches through recent 2015-2025 developments. Five theory families are examined: (1) Classical/Equivalent Single-Layer (ESL) theories (CPT, FSDT, HSDT), which treat the sandwich as a homogenized continuum; (2) Frostig's High-Order Sandwich Panel Theory (HSAPT), which models the core as a 2D elastic medium with non-linear through-thickness displacement fields and explicitly separates face-sheet kinematics; (3) Extended High-Order Sandwich Panel Theory (EHSAPT) by Kardomateas et al., which adds core in-plane rigidity and full compressibility; (4) Theories that decouple global bending from local layer bending, including layerwise, zigzag (Murakami's MZZF, Tessler's RZT), and global-local enrichment schemes; and (5) Key 2015-2025 advances encompassing Carrera Unified Formulation (CUF) with variable kinematics, non-polynomial HSDTs, IGA implementations, machine-learning/PINN surrogates, and multi-physics extensions. Each framework's core assumptions, governing kinematic fields, and recognized limitations are detailed.

### Classical Laminate Plate Theory (CLPT) and First-Order Shear Deformation Theory (FSDT)

**假设**: CLPT: (a) Kirchhoff hypothesis -- normals to the mid-plane remain straight, inextensible, and normal after deformation; (b) transverse shear strains γ_xz, γ_yz = 0; (c) plane-stress constitutive behavior per layer; (d) perfect bonding between layers; (e) small strains and displacements. FSDT (Mindlin-Reissner): relaxes assumption (a) to allow constant transverse shear strain through the thickness; requires a shear correction factor (typically k = 5/6). Both treat the sandwich as a single equivalent layer (ESL) via through-thickness homogenization of stiffness matrices (ABD matrix).

**局限**: CLPT neglects transverse shear entirely -- produces errors exceeding 30% for span-to-thickness ratios below 20 in soft-core sandwiches and overpredicts buckling loads. FSDT captures constant shear but requires an ad hoc correction factor that depends on lamination sequence; errors of 10-15% for moderately thick sandwiches. Neither theory captures the zigzag displacement pattern at stiff-face/soft-core interfaces, nor do they account for transverse normal stress in the core. Transverse shear stresses from constitutive equations are discontinuous at layer interfaces -- post-processing via 3D equilibrium integration is required.

**关键文献**: Sayyad & Ghugal (2016), Composite Structures, 184, 192-211. Reddy, J.N. (2004), Mechanics of Laminated Composite Plates and Shells, 2nd ed., CRC Press.

### Higher-Order Shear Deformation Theories (HSDT) including Reddy's Third-Order Theory (TSDT)

**假设**: (a) In-plane displacements expanded as cubic (or higher) polynomials in the thickness coordinate z; (b) transverse displacement w is constant through thickness (w = w₀); (c) zero transverse shear traction condition on top and bottom surfaces is enforced a priori (τ_xz, τ_yz = 0 at z = ±h/2); (d) parabolic distribution of transverse shear strains -- no shear correction factor required; (e) linear elastic material, small strains, ESL homogenization. Reddy's TSDT field: u = u₀ + z·ψ_x − (4/(3h²))·z³·(ψ_x + dw₀/dx).

**局限**: While eliminating the shear correction factor, HSDT still uses ESL homogenization -- it cannot capture the C₀-discontinuity of in-plane displacement slopes at layer interfaces (no true zigzag). Transverse normal stress σ_zz is neglected. Errors of 5-18% for thick soft-core sandwiches in deflection and stress prediction. Polynomial-order truncation limits accuracy for very thick or highly heterogeneous cores. Non-polynomial HSDTs (trigonometric, hyperbolic, exponential) developed post-2015 improve accuracy but share the fundamental ESL limitation.

**关键文献**: Reddy, J.N. (1984), J. Applied Mechanics, 51(4), 745-752. Thai & Kim (2015), Composite Structures, 128, 70-86.

### Frostig's High-Order Sandwich Panel Theory (HSAPT)

**假设**: (a) Face sheets: each modeled as independent Euler-Bernoulli or Timoshenko beams/plates with separate displacement fields (u_t, w_t) and (u_b, w_b); (b) Core: modeled as a 2D elastic continuum (NOT an anti-plane spring layer) with full in-plane and transverse stiffness; core displacement field derived by solving the 2D elasticity equilibrium equations, yielding a non-linear polynomial distribution through the thickness; (c) Full interfacial displacement continuity at face-core boundaries (no slip, no delamination); (d) Transverse normal stress σ_zz is non-zero and varies through the core thickness -- captures core compressibility (breathing modes); (e) Core shear stress τ_xz is non-uniform through thickness, computed from equilibrium; (f) Small strains, linear elasticity, isotropic or orthotropic core; (g) Flat/straight panel geometry.

**局限**: Limited to linearly elastic, small-strain behavior -- geometric nonlinearities require extensions with von Kármán kinematics. Assumes perfect bonding (no interfacial compliance or debonding). Computational cost significantly higher than ESL theories due to additional DOFs. Accuracy depends on the polynomial order chosen for core in-plane displacement -- quadratic fields may be insufficient for very thick cores or highly localized loading. Core in-plane stress influence can be overestimated in very stiff cores. Basic formulation is for flat panels only; curved geometries require reformulation. No inherent thermal/hygral effects.

**关键文献**: Frostig, Baruch, Vilnay, & Sheinman (1992), J. Engineering Mechanics, ASCE, 118(5), 1026-1043. Frostig & Thomsen (2004), Int. J. Solids and Structures, 41(9-10), 2797-2824.

### Extended High-Order Sandwich Panel Theory (EHSAPT) -- Kardomateas et al.

**假设**: Builds on HSAPT with these additions: (a) Core in-plane (axial) rigidity is explicitly included -- the core is treated as a full 2D elastic medium with in-plane stiffness components C₁₁, C₁₃, C₃₃, not just transverse shear and normal stiffness; (b) Core displacement fields derived from 2D elasticity equilibrium with full constitutive coupling between σ_xx and σ_zz through Poisson's ratio; (c) Compressibility (ε_zz ≠ 0, σ_zz ≠ 0) is a fundamental feature, enabling accurate modeling of core crushing and indentation; (d) Both face sheets and core satisfy their respective 2D elasticity equilibrium equations; (e) Interfacial continuity of displacements enforced. Extensions to curved panels, geometric nonlinearity (von Kármán), and finite element formulations developed 2015-2017 by Yuan, Kardomateas, and Frostig.

**局限**: Higher computational cost than HSAPT due to additional core constitutive terms. Assumes linear elastic core material -- viscoelasticity, plasticity, and damage not included in the base formulation. Perfect bonding only. The polynomial representation of core in-plane displacement may require higher-order terms for extreme softness ratios or highly concentrated loads. Curved panel formulation adds complexity in curvilinear coordinates. Limited implementations in commercial FE codes -- requires specialized elements. Validation against full 3D elasticity solutions is still sparse for certain loading configurations.

**关键文献**: Phan, Kardomateas, & Frostig (2012), J. Applied Mechanics (ASME), 79(6), 061003. Yuan, Kardomateas, & Frostig (2016), J. Applied Mechanics, 83(9), 091008.

### Murakami Zigzag Function (MZZF) Theory

**假设**: (a) The ESL displacement field is augmented by a piecewise linear zigzag function: uᵢ = uᵢ^ESL(x,y,z) + ψᵢ(x,y)·F_ZZ(z), where F_ZZ(z) = (−1)^k·z_k (sign-based zigzag in each layer k); (b) The zigzag function introduces C₀-discontinuity in in-plane displacement slopes at interfaces; (c) DOES NOT depend on layer material properties -- purely kinematic/mathematical augmentation; (d) Same number of DOFs as the base ESL theory plus two zigzag amplitudes ψ_x, ψ_y; (e) Can be combined with any ESL theory within the Carrera Unified Formulation (CUF) framework.

**局限**: Transverse normal stress σ_zz remains poorly captured -- MZZF addresses in-plane zigzag only. Interlaminar transverse stress continuity is not inherently satisfied; post-processing via 3D equilibrium integration is required. Requires at least cubic/quartic thickness expansion order for good results in sandwich applications. The sign-function approach is arbitrary and not derived from physical equilibrium conditions. Transverse shear stress from constitutive equations can still be inaccurate (~15% error) without post-processing. Less accurate than the full Refined Zigzag Theory (RZT) for highly heterogeneous sandwiches because MZZF does not use layer stiffness ratios.

**关键文献**: Murakami, H. (1986), J. Applied Mechanics, 53(3), 661-666. Carrera, E. (2003), Archives of Computational Methods in Engineering, 10(3), 215-296.

### Refined Zigzag Theory (RZT) -- Tessler et al.

**假设**: (a) Kinematic field: u^(k) = u₀ − z·(dw₀/dx) + φ_x^(k)(z)·ψ_x(x,y), w = w₀ (7 DOFs total for plates: u₀, v₀, w₀, θ_x, θ_y, ψ_x, ψ_y); (b) Zigzag function φ_α^(k)(z) is piecewise linear, uniquely determined from layer transverse shear moduli ratios β_α^(k) = G_αz/G_αz^(k) − 1; (c) Zigzag function constrained to have zero thickness-average to decouple from average bending rotations; (d) C₀-continuity of in-plane displacements and transverse shear stresses; (e) Transverse normal stress σ_zz is neglected in the original RZT; (f) DOF count is independent of the number of layers.

**局限**: The original RZT (2007-2010) neglects transverse normal deformation (ε_zz = 0), so core compressibility effects are not captured -- critical for soft-core indentation and crushing problems. RZT(m) extensions for through-thickness stretching exist but increase complexity. Zigzag function derivation assumes linear elastic layer behavior. Requires C₀-continuous finite elements. For highly localized loading, post-processing of σ_zz via equilibrium integration is still needed. Accuracy degrades for extreme face-to-core modulus ratios unless higher-order zigzag functions are used. Thermomechanical and piezoelectric coupling require separate RZT extensions.

**关键文献**: Tessler, Di Sciuva, & Gherlone (2009), J. Composite Materials, 43(9), 1051-1081. Tessler, Di Sciuva, & Gherlone (2010), J. Mechanics of Materials and Structures, 5(2), 341-367.

### Global-Local Decoupling Theories (Layerwise and Selective Enrichment)

**假设**: The total response is the superposition of a global (smeared/ESL) component and a local (layer-by-layer) correction. Implementation strategies include: (a) Full Layerwise theories (Reddy, Barbero): each layer has independent displacement fields; C₀-continuity enforced at interfaces; accuracy approaches 3D elasticity. (b) Global-local layerwise: ESL kinematics for global bending plus layerwise enrichment selectively applied in zones with flexible cores or high gradients. (c) Quasi-3D layerwise: includes thickness stretching (ε_zz ≠ 0) through higher-order expansions in w per layer. (d) Carrera Unified Formulation (CUF): systematic framework where the order and type of thickness expansion (Taylor, Legendre, Lagrange) can be selected per-layer -- enables variable kinematic models.

**局限**: Full layerwise theories: DOFs scale linearly with number of layers -- computationally prohibitive for many-layer laminates. Global-local methods: require a priori knowledge of which zones need enrichment; transition between ESL and layerwise regions introduces artificial interfaces. Quasi-3D: additional DOFs from thickness stretching increase cost. CUF: while elegant and general, requires specialized codes (not in commercial FE); the choice of optimal expansion order per layer is problem-dependent and not automated. All global-local methods struggle with delamination and interfacial damage unless complemented with cohesive-zone or fracture mechanics models.

**关键文献**: Carrera & Brischetto (2009), Applied Mechanics Reviews, 62(1), 010803. Liew, Pan, & Zhang (2019), Composite Structures, 216, 240-259.

### Isogeometric Analysis (IGA) with Higher-Order Sandwich Kinematics (2021-2024)

**假设**: (a) Displacement fields from HSDT, zigzag, or layerwise theories are discretized using NURBS basis functions that provide C¹-continuity (or higher) of the geometry and displacement fields; (b) Exact geometry representation for curved sandwich shells; (c) Higher inter-element continuity eliminates shear-locking without reduced integration; (d) Typically combined with HSDT or RZT kinematics rather than full 3D; (e) Layerwise IGA models treat each layer's geometry independently via multi-patch NURBS.

**局限**: NURBS tensor-product structure limits local refinement; T-splines or hierarchical B-splines are needed for adaptive refinement but add implementation complexity. IGA with layerwise kinematics still has DOFs scaling with layers. Computational cost is higher than standard FEM for the same mesh density. Limited to problems with parameterizable geometry. Few IGA sandwich codes are publicly available. Validation against standard FE benchmarks is ongoing; error behavior for extreme face-to-core stiffness ratios is not fully characterized.

**关键文献**: Nguyen-Xuan et al. (2022). Isogeometric analysis of functionally graded and sandwich plates: A review. Composite Structures, 280, 114896.

### Machine Learning and Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Sandwich Structures (2022-2025)

**假设**: (a) Training data generated from high-fidelity layerwise or 3D FE models; (b) Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) embed the governing PDEs (equilibrium, constitutive, compatibility) into the loss function, enabling solution without labeled training data; (c) Surrogate models learn the input-output mapping from load/geometry/material parameters to displacement/stress response; (d) Reduced-order models use Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) or autoencoders on FE solution snapshots; (e) Zigzag/layerwise kinematics may be embedded into the network architecture as inductive bias.

**局限**: PINNs struggle with the multi-scale nature of sandwich structures -- the stiff-face/soft-core contrast creates high solution gradients that are hard to capture with a single network. Training cost for 3D sandwich problems often exceeds the cost of a single FE solve; the advantage only materializes for many-query problems (optimization, uncertainty quantification). Surrogate accuracy depends heavily on the training data distribution; extrapolation to unseen geometries is unreliable. Standard PINNs with L₂ loss struggle to satisfy traction boundary conditions exactly. No ML-based sandwich theories have been validated for certification-level analysis. The black-box nature limits adoption in safety-critical applications.

**关键文献**: Samaniego et al. (2020). An energy approach to the solution of PDEs in computational mechanics via machine learning. CMAME, 362, 112790.

### 与该问题的关联

The five focus areas map directly to the hierarchy of increasing fidelity in sandwich structural analysis. (1) Classical vs. High-Order: The fundamental choice -- ESL theories (CLPT/FSDT/HSDT) are computationally cheap but fail for soft-core sandwiches (30%+ deflection errors). Frostig's HSAPT and related theories are essential when the core cannot be treated as an anti-plane spring. (2) Frostig's HSAPT: The canonical high-order theory for soft-core sandwiches, and the conceptual ancestor of most modern formulations. Its treatment of the core as a 2D elastic continuum with independent face-sheet kinematics is the gold-standard paradigm. (3) Core Compressibility (EHSAPT): Critical when transverse normal stresses matter -- indentation under concentrated loads, core crushing, wrinkling instability. EHSAPT adds in-plane core rigidity to HSAPT, closing the gap between 2D sandwich theories and full 3D elasticity for these failure modes. (4) Decoupled Global-Local Bending: This is the computational-efficiency challenge. RZT (Tessler) achieves near-layerwise accuracy with only 7 DOFs total. MZZF (Murakami) provides a simpler kinematic patch. CUF (Carrera) offers a systematic framework for variable-fidelity models. The choice depends on whether the application needs stress accuracy at interfaces (use RZT or full layerwise) or only global displacements (use MZZF-enhanced HSDT). (5) 2015-2025 Developments: The field is maturing from analytical formulations toward computational implementation: IGA provides locking-free discretization, ML/PINN methods promise real-time surrogates for optimization, and multi-physics extensions bring the theories closer to engineering practice. The unresolved frontier is certifiable accuracy for safety-critical applications and standardized benchmarks for the new computational methods.

## 3. 板-膜过渡机制

The "plate-to-membrane transition" describes the shift in structural behavior from bending-dominated (plate regime) to stretching-dominated (membrane regime) as geometry, loading, or boundary conditions change. This transition arises across a remarkably broad set of disciplines — from classical plate theory and sandwich panel mechanics to lattice metamaterials, biological composites (nacre), and statistical physics of 2D crystalline membranes. The core unifying concept is a dimensionless parameter comparing bending stiffness to extensional/membrane stiffness at a given length scale. The most fundamental is the Föppl–von Kármán number γ = Y·L²/κ (where Y is the 2D stretching modulus and κ is the bending rigidity), but each subfield has specialized equivalents: Allen's s-parameter for sandwich beams, the Faulkner β slenderness parameter for ship panels, the Maxwell connectivity criterion for lattice topologies, and the shear-lag aspect-ratio parameter for nacre-like composites. Four major families of regime maps exist: (1) Ashby-style mechanism maps for sandwich beams (bending/core-shear/indentation), (2) topological phase diagrams for lattices (Maxwell criterion boundary), (3) effective-width/slenderness diagrams for post-buckled stiffened panels, and (4) "Best Plate Diagrams" from the Carrera Unified Formulation that identify the minimum kinematic model order needed. The concept of "bending degrees of freedom activation" refers to the hierarchical unlocking of rotational kinematic freedoms in layered media — in sandwich panels this means the transition from Kirchhoff (no shear DOF) to Mindlin to layerwise zig-zag kinematics; in nacre it means the progressive engagement of tablet rotation and interlamellar sliding versus rigid platelet behavior. Key open questions include the precise nature of the transition band (smooth versus abrupt crossover), coupling between multiple length scales in hierarchical composites, and experimental validation of regime boundaries predicted by asymptotic homogenization.

### 无量纲参数

- **Föppl–von Kármán number (γ)**: γ = Y·L²/κ = 12(1−ν²)L²/h², where Y = E·h is the 2D stretching modulus, κ = E·h³/[12(1−ν²)] is the bending rigidity, L is a characteristic lateral length scale, h is thickness, ν is Poisson's ratio — Ratio of stretching energy to bending energy in a thin elastic sheet. γ ≪ 10³ → bending-dominated harmonic regime; γ ~ 10³–10⁵ → anomalous flat phase with fluctuation-stiffened bending rigidity; γ > 10⁶ → crumpled/collapsed membrane phase. Controls the plate-to-membrane crossover in both continuum mechanics and statistical physics of 2D membranes (e.g., graphene thermal rippling).

- **Allen's shear parameter (s)**: s = π²·E_f·t_f·d / (G_c·L²) where E_f = face-sheet modulus, t_f = face thickness, d = face-sheet separation distance, G_c = core shear modulus, L = span — Ratio of sandwich beam bending stiffness to core shear stiffness over a given span. s → ∞ means classical bending theory applies; s → 0 means shear deformation dominates and the sandwich acts like a shear beam. Deflection correction factor is (1 + 1/s). Fundamental to classifying whether a sandwich panel is in the thick-plate, thin-plate, or membrane regime.

- **Plate slenderness parameter (β)**: β = (b/t)·√(σ_y/E) where b = plate width between stiffeners, t = plate thickness, σ_y = yield stress, E = Young's modulus — Controls the post-buckling effective width and the transition from bending-dominated buckling to membrane-tension-dominated post-buckling reserve strength in stiffened panels. β < 1: plate yields before buckling (membrane-strength limited); 1 < β < 3: post-buckling strength governed by effective width; β > 3: slender plate, membrane action develops fully after early buckling.

- **Maxwell connectivity number (M)**: M = b − 3j + 6 (3D) or M = b − 2j + 3 (2D), where b = number of struts, j = number of joints — Topological criterion for stretching-dominated versus bending-dominated lattice behavior. M ≥ 0 → stretching-dominated (stiffness ∝ ρ); M < 0 → bending-dominated (stiffness ∝ ρ² or ρ³). The structural analog of Maxwell's rule for rigidity percolation.

- **Normalized deflection (λ)**: λ = w_max / h, where w_max is the maximum transverse deflection and h is the plate thickness — Criterion for geometric nonlinearity onset. λ ≪ 1: linear bending (Kirchhoff plate theory sufficient). λ ~ 1: nonlinear coupling begins; von Kármán equations required; membrane stretching contributes comparably to bending. λ ≫ 1: membrane-dominated regime; load-deflection curves transition from bending-linear to membrane-cubic scaling.

- **Bending-to-membrane coupling ratio (B/√(AD))**: B_ij / √(A_ij·D_ij) from classical lamination theory, where [A] is the extensional stiffness matrix, [B] is the extension-bending coupling matrix, [D] is the bending stiffness matrix — Quantifies the inherent coupling between membrane and bending deformation in asymmetric laminates. When B ≠ 0, pure bending induces membrane strains and vice versa, blurring the transition boundary and providing a continuous coupling pathway between regimes.

- **Shear-lag effectiveness coefficient (k)**: k = σ_actual / σ_uniform ≤ 1, or equivalently k = b_e / b where b_e is the effective width. For nacre: k ∝ tanh(λ_s·L)/(λ_s·L) with λ_s ∝ √(G_m/(E_p·h_p·t_m)) — Measures how uniformly stress is transferred across a wide structural element through interfacial shear. k → 1: full section participates (membrane-action efficient). k ≪ 1: stress localizes near edges, center carries little load (transition to bending-localized behavior). In nacre, governs the critical platelet aspect ratio for optimal load transfer.

- **Kohn-Vogelius corrugation transition parameter**: h_c²/(λ·t), where h_c = corrugation amplitude, λ = corrugation wavelength, t = sheet thickness — Separates bending-dominated (shallow corrugation, parameter ≪ 1) from membrane-dominated (deep corrugation, parameter ≫ 1) effective behavior in periodically corrugated plates after homogenization. Derived from asymptotic expansion in sheet thickness.

- **Load-geometry parameter (Π)**: Π = q·L⁴/(E·h⁴) where q = lateral pressure, L = characteristic span, E = Young's modulus, h = thickness — Normalized lateral load. Π ≪ O(10): linear plate regime (bending). Π ~ O(10)–O(10³): nonlinear transition. Π ≫ O(10³): membrane-dominated large deflection. Used to collapse load-deflection data onto universal curves for different geometries.

- **Föppl, A. (1907)** — Original formulation of the nonlinear plate equations that couple bending and membrane action. The Föppl–von Kármán equations remain the foundational PDE system governing the plate-to-membrane transition.
- **von Kármán, T. (1910)** — Completed the large-deflection plate theory bearing his name. Later (1924) introduced the effective-width concept for post-buckled plates, quantifying how membrane tension redistributes stress after buckling.
- **Allen, H.G. (1969)** — Comprehensive treatise on sandwich panel mechanics. Defined the s-parameter that governs shear-deformation contributions to sandwich beam deflection, establishing the bending-to-shear stiffness ratio as the key dimensionless group for sandwich regimes.
- **Gao, H., Ji, B., Jäger, I.L., Arzt, E., & Fratzl, P. (2003)** — Introduced the tension-shear chain model for nacre in PNAS. Showed that below a critical length scale (~30 nm), mineral crystals become flaw-tolerant, enabling a brittle-to-ductile transition. Shear-lag mechanics governs platelet stress transfer and aspect ratio optimization.
- **Deshpande, V.S., Fleck, N.A., & Ashby, M.F. (2001)** — Classified lattice materials into bending-dominated and stretching-dominated topologies using the Maxwell stability criterion (Acta Materialia). Established stiffness scaling laws: E* ∝ ρ for stretching-dominated, E* ∝ ρ² for bending-dominated. Created material property charts for cellular solids.
- **Barthelat, F., Tang, H., Zavattieri, P.D., Li, C.M., & Espinosa, H.D. (2007)** — Detailed experimental-numerical study of nacre mechanics (JMPS). Characterized interlamellar sliding, platelet rotation (bending degrees of freedom), and progressive strain hardening. Measured interface shear strength and pull-out work.
- **Carrera, E. (2003)** — Proposed the Carrera Unified Formulation (CUF) for hierarchical plate/shell theories. Enabled 'Best Plate Diagrams' that map dimensionless parameters (a/h, E₁/E₂) to the minimum required kinematic expansion order, systematically classifying when bending DOFs beyond Kirchhoff are needed.
- **Nelson, D.R., Piran, T., & Weinberg, S. (eds.) (2004)** — Statistical Mechanics of Membranes and Surfaces. Established the Föppl–von Kármán number γ as the control parameter for the flat-to-crumpled phase transition in 2D crystalline membranes. Predicted universal roughness and bending rigidity renormalization exponents (η ≈ 0.821, ζ ≈ 0.590).
- **Fasolino, A., Los, J.H., & Katsnelson, M.I. (2007)** — Demonstrated via atomistic Monte Carlo (Nature Materials) that graphene at room temperature exists in the anomalous flat phase of FvK theory, with thermal ripples of ~1 nm amplitude and a roughness exponent ζ ≈ 0.6. Connected statistical membrane physics to experimentally observable material behavior.
- **Kohn, R.V. & Vogelius, M. (1984)** — Asymptotic homogenization of periodically corrugated plates. Derived effective stiffness tensors and identified the dimensionless parameter h_c²/(λ·t) that governs the bending-to-membrane transition in corrugated structures.
- **Faulkner, D. (1975)** — Proposed the b_e/b = 2/β − 1/β² formula for effective width of post-buckled ship plating (Journal of Ship Research). Comprehensive framework for stiffened panel ultimate strength including Smith's progressive collapse method, capturing the bending-to-membrane transition in hull plating through average stress-strain curves that account for effective width evolution.

### 已有模型

- **Föppl–von Kármán equations** (1907/1910): Nonlinear PDE system coupling the biharmonic bending equation with a membrane stress function via Gaussian curvature. The foundational continuum model governing the plate-to-membrane transition through geometric nonlinearity.
- **Shear-lag / tension-shear chain model** (Gao et al., 2003): 1D load-transfer model for staggered brick-and-mortar composites. Stress transfers between stiff platelets via shear in the compliant matrix. Predicts an optimal platelet aspect ratio for maximizing load transfer while avoiding brittle fracture.
- **Allen's sandwich beam theory** (1969): Equations for sandwich beam deflection including both bending and shear deformation terms. The s-parameter explicitly controls the bending/shear mix. Total deflection = w_bending × (1 + constant/s).
- **Effective-width methods** (von Kármán 1924; Faulkner 1975; Eurocode 3-1-5): Semi-empirical models that replace the actual plate width with a reduced effective width after buckling, capturing the plate-to-membrane transition in post-buckling reserve strength.
- **Maxwell lattice classification** (Deshpande et al., 2001): Topological criterion M = b − d·j + d(d+1)/2 that predicts whether a lattice is stretching-dominated (M ≥ 0) or bending-dominated (M < 0). Links topology to mechanical scaling exponents.
- **Carrera Unified Formulation** (CUF, 2003): Hierarchical framework for deriving plate/shell theories of any order from a single compact notation. Enables systematic mapping of which kinematic model (Kirchhoff, Mindlin, Reddy, layerwise) is needed as a function of slenderness and orthotropy.
- **FvK statistical mechanics of membranes** (Nelson et al., 1989/2004): Renormalization group treatment of thermal fluctuations in 2D crystalline membranes. Predicts scale-dependent bending rigidity κ_R(q) ~ q^(−η) and a crumpling transition at a critical FvK number γ_c.
- **Kohn-Vogelius homogenization** (1984): Asymptotic expansion method for periodically corrugated plates yielding effective plate/membrane stiffness tensors. Identifies the dimensionless parameter controlling the regime transition.

### 研究空白

Several fundamental gaps remain in the unified understanding of the plate-to-membrane transition: (1) The precise nature of the crossover — whether it is a sharp second-order-like transition or a broad crossover band — is not fully characterized for many layered composite systems, especially in the presence of interfacial nonlinearity (friction, damage, viscoelasticity). (2) While the Föppl–von Kármán number and its analogs are well-established in their respective subfields, there is no general framework that unifies the transition criteria across the very different physical systems (sandwich panels, nacre, lattices, stiffened plates, corrugated sheets, and fluctuating 2D membranes) into a common phase-diagram formalism. (3) The coupling between multiple length scales in hierarchical composites (e.g., nacre with nano-asperities on micron-scale tablets in millimeter-thick shells) means the transition can occur simultaneously at different scales with complex interactions — existing models treat each scale mostly independently. (4) Experimental regime maps for the transition (e.g., direct measurement of the bending-to-membrane strain energy ratio as a function of load and geometry) are scarce outside of lattice metamaterials. (5) The concept of 'bending degrees of freedom activation' is well-developed in computational plate theory (CUF diagrams) but lacks a corresponding experimental framework — there are no standard test protocols that quantify how many kinematic DOFs are 'active' in a given layered structure under a given load. (6) The role of interfacial compliance in mediating the plate-to-membrane transition — particularly in nacre-like and synthetic layered composites — has not been reduced to a simple dimensionless parameter that engineers can use for design, analogous to the s-parameter in sandwich theory.
